In 1962, a single laugh triggered a contagious epidemic across an entire African region. It started at a Tanzanian boarding school when three girls suddenly couldn’t stop giggling. Within weeks, ninety-five students were infected, forcing authorities to permanently close the campus. The laughter spread to nearby villages, leaving thousands paralyzed by painful, breathless cackling. Medical teams arrived baffled, watching victims laugh until they collapsed from sheer exhaustion. Researchers diagnosed mass psychogenic illness, born from intense academic pressure and cultural upheaval. After months of isolation and community therapy, the epidemic finally broke without a trace. History’s strangest outbreak proves how deeply unspoken stress can physically manifest in crowds.
