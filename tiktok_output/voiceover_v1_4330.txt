In the early 1830s, a tiny nation sprang up in New Hampshire. Sandwiched between the U.S. and British Canada, its borders were a mystery. Treaty of Paris left the Connecticut River's head ambiguous, sparking conflict. On July 9, 1832, 56 locals voted for their own constitution. The new Republic boasted a bill of rights, militia, and taxes. Both nations tried to tax and draft its citizens, causing tension. In 1835, New Hampshire threatened militia; the Republic surrendered. By 1842, the Webster‐Ashburton Treaty finally settled the border. Today, the area is Pittsburg, NH, but the story lives on.
