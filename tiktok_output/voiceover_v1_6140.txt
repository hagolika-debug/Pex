In the early 1800s, a tiny borderland declared itself a nation. Located along the Connecticut River, Indian Stream sat between the U.S. and British Canada. Conflicting land grants and double taxes left residents angry and confused. On July 9, 1832, they proclaimed independence and drafted a constitution. The fledgling republic elected a council, held courts, and even printed its own stamps. Tensions rose when New Hampshire sheriffs tried to collect debts, sparking a militia standoff. By August 5, 1835, the republic capitulated and was annexed by New Hampshire. The 1842 Webster‐Ashburton Treaty finally settled the border, erasing the forgotten nation.
