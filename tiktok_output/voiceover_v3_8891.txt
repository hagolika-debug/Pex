In 1986, a silent lake in Cameroon killed nearly two thousand people overnight. No earthquake. No explosion. Just a sudden, invisible cloud rolling down the valley. Deep beneath the water, volcanic carbon dioxide had been building for decades. A landslide finally cracked the seal, releasing a suffocating wave heavier than air. It moved at highway speeds, extinguishing every flame and breath in its path. Survivors woke to absolute silence, finding entire villages untouched but completely lifeless. Scientists now monitor the lake, pumping gas to prevent history from repeating itself. Sometimes the deadliest disasters never roar. They simply exhale, then vanish.
