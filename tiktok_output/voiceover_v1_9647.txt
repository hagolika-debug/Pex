In 1835, a newspaper convinced the world life thrived on the moon. The New York Sun published exclusive reports from a famous British astronomer. Readers gasped at descriptions of sapphire temples, grazing unicorns, and winged humanoids. Churches held midnight vigils. Scientists debated. The paper sold out daily. But the astronomer never visited the moon. He was in South Africa. A reporter named Richard Adams Locke had fabricated the entire discovery. He wanted to mock wild scientific theories and boost failing circulation. The hoax worked perfectly, birthing modern media sensationalism overnight.
