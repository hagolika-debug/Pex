In 1841[0.8s] Rhode Island, voting required owning forty pounds of land. Most white men were locked out[1s] sparking a fierce reform movement. Thomas Dorr drafted a People's Constitution[0.5s] promising universal male suffrage. Two rival governments emerged, each claiming legitimacy over the state. Dorr's militia seized the state arsenal[1.2s] but their cannons misfired spectacularly. The showdown never happened; no battle, only a single civilian casualty. After weeks of standoffs, Dorr fled, then returned to rally supporters. The rebellion collapsed, but forced Rhode Island to rewrite its constitution. Legacy[0.8s]: Dorr became a martyr for voting rights, inspiring future reforms.
