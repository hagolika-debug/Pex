January 1919, Boston’s North End turned into a sugary nightmare. A 58‐foot steel tank burst, unleashing 2.3 million gallons. The viscous wave surged at 35 mph[1s], crushing everything. Residents were trapped, some suffocated, others swept away. Rescue crews fought through sticky quicksand, pulling survivors from the mess[0.5s]. The tragedy claimed 21 lives and injured over 150. Lawsuits followed; the company paid $628,000 in damages. Even today, the scent of molasses lingered over Boston. A sweet disaster reminds us: engineering shortcuts can turn deadly[1s].
