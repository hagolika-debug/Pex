In 1919, Boston woke to a tidal wave of syrup. A massive steel tank burst, releasing 2.3 million gallons. The sticky surge raced 35 miles per hour[1s], crushing everything. Residents were trapped, horses drowned, buildings turned to paste. First responders waded through thick goo[0.5s], rescuing survivors. The city sued the company, blaming shoddy construction. A $628,000 settlement finally paid victims—equivalent to millions today. Even weeks later, the air smelled of caramel[0.8s].
