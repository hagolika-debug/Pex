Picture a tiny, forgotten nation wedged between the US and Canada. The Indian Stream Republic existed from July 9, 1832 until August 5, 1835. Its birth sprang from the ambiguous 1783 Treaty of Paris border language. Roughly three hundred settlers created an elected assembly and written constitution. Both British and American sheriffs taxed them, prompting fierce local resistance. To stop double taxation, they declared independence and raised their own flag. In 1835 New Hampshire militia marched in, forcing the republic’s annexation. A petty hardware-store debt almost ignited a diplomatic crisis between nations. The 1842 Webster‐Ashburton Treaty finally resolved the border, ending the republic.
