Imagine a tiny nation that existed for just three years. Born from a blurry 1783 border[0.5s] between the U.S. and Canada. Nestled in today’s New Hampshire, along the Indian Stream valley. Both America and Britain sent tax collectors[1s], sparking double‐tax chaos. On July 9, 1832, locals drafted a constitution and declared independence. They elected a justice, Luther Parker, to lead the fledgling republic. New Hampshire militia[0.8s] threatened invasion; the Republic faced armed standoff. By August 1835, the militia entered, and the Republic surrendered. The dispute lingered until the 1842 Webster‐Ashburton Treaty settled the border. Today, the lost nation lives in markers, reminding us borders can be fragile.[1s]
