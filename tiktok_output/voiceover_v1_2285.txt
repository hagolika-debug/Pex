In 1962, a mysterious laughter outbreak swept Tanganyika's schools. Just months after independence, students faced strict colonial discipline. Three teenage girls burst into uncontrollable giggles, sparking chaos. Within days, dozens of students couldn't stop laughing, halting lessons. Officials shut the school, hoping to contain the strange epidemic. But laughter traveled home, infecting villages across the region. Doctors found no virus, labeling it mass psychogenic illness. Schools closed for months, disrupting education and daily life. As tensions eased, the giggles faded and normalcy returned. The epidemic shows stress can erupt in the strangest ways.
