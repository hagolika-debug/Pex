Britain’s first WWII air casualty wasn’t shot down by Nazis. He was killed by his own side. September 1939. Britain just declared war. Radar is new. Pilots are jumpy. Friendly aircraft appear on radar. No IFF signals. Pilots scramble to intercept... unknowns. They open fire. Down goes Flight Lieutenant Caesar Hull. A national air-racing hero. Dead. Officials called it ‘The Battle of Barking Creek.’ Made it sound heroic. Buried the truth. Turns out, the ‘enemy’ planes were British. The kill was friendly fire. And the pilot who died? He’d just survived the Battle of Britain... in a different plane. Hull left a wife and infant daughter. His squadron knew. The public never did. Until decades later. Post-war records revealed the truth. Today, a humble plaque in Essex honors him. Not as a battlefield hero—but as a victim of panic. The fog of war isn’t just smoke and shells.
