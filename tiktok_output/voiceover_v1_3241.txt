In 1832, a tiny New Hampshire border village declared independence. The cause? Confusing Treaty of Paris borders left them taxed twice. Locals formed a constitution, electing Luther Parker as justice. The Republic spanned the four Connecticut Lakes, home to 300 settlers. Both US sheriffs and British officials tried collecting taxes, sparking tension. In 1835, New Hampshire militia marched in, demanding surrender. The assembly capitulated; Indian Stream was annexed by New Hampshire. The border dispute finally settled in 1842 by the Webster‐Ashburton Treaty. Today, the area is Pittsburg, New Hampshire—still whispering its rebel past.
