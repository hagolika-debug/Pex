Imagine an army that defeats itself without firing a single enemy shot. In September 1788, Austria marched into Transylvania during the Austro‐Turkish War. Hundred thousand troops set up camp near Caransebeș, nerves already frayed. Hussars bought schnapps from locals, but infantry demanded their share, tensions rose. A shouted “Turci!” sparked panic; soldiers mistook allies for Ottoman attackers. Gunfire echoed; Austrian regiments fired into each other, artillery joined the chaos. Emperor Joseph II, lost in the night, ordered a chaotic retreat. Two days later, real Turkish forces marched in, finding a shattered camp. Historians call it history's worst friendly‐fire disaster, with thousands wounded. The incident inspired the phrase ‘fighting oneself’ in military slang.
