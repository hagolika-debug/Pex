In 1901, Ghana's capital erupted in a soldier's uprising. British troops withheld months of pay, sparking mounting resentment among native soldiers. On March 18, mutineers opened fire on British officers in Kumasi. The clash lasted three weeks, with skirmishes echoing through the city. Twelve mutineers fell, while dozens more vanished after the fighting. Survivors were court‐martialed, many sentenced to hard labor in Sierra Leone. The British press barely mentioned it, burying the revolt in history. Today, Kumasi remembers the mutiny as a symbol of colonial resistance.
