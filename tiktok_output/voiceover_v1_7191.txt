In 1832, a tiny nation sprang up on a New Hampshire border. Confusing Treaty of Paris boundaries left the land claimed by both US and Britain. On July 9, locals declared independence, drafting their own constitution. They elected officials, even a justice of the peace named Luther Parker. Three hundred settlers lived among the serene Connecticut Lakes, paying double taxes. Sheriff from New Hampshire demanded control, leading to militia mobilization. A tense standoff at Stewartstown ended with the Republic’s surrender. By August 5, 1835, New Hampshire annexed Indian Stream, ending its brief freedom. Today, the story lives on in Pittsburg, NH’s historic marker.
